1\. Working Strategic Direction

Spotsylvania RFC should be positioned as a community-based youth rugby club serious about both athletic development and character formation.

The club should not sound like a generic youth activity, casual recreation program, elite travel-sports machine, private-school-only program, or adult rugby club that happens to have youth offerings.

The club should be local, disciplined, family-oriented, serious, welcoming, and durable.

Core Strategic Position  
Spotsylvania RFC is a rugby club in formation serving the Spotsylvania–Fredericksburg region, beginning with non-contact youth rugby and building carefully toward a durable local rugby pathway.

Public One-Sentence Version  
Spotsylvania RFC is a community-based rugby club in formation, beginning with safe non-contact youth rugby and building a long-term pathway for local players, families, coaches, and supporters.

Expanded One-Sentence Brand Architecture  
Spotsylvania Rugby Football Club, publicly known as Spotsylvania RFC, is a traditional rugby club in formation, beginning with Spotsylvania Youth Rugby and Spotsylvania Tag Rugby as safe non-contact entry points, guided publicly by Unity · Courage · Service, rallied by the founding phrase Put Your Hand Up, and supported by a secondary heritage language of formation, durability, and being Forged in Spotsylvania.

3\. Mission, Vision, and Purpose

Mission  
Spotsylvania Rugby Football Club exists to introduce, develop, and sustain rugby in the Spotsylvania–Fredericksburg region by forming young athletes through skillful play, disciplined competition, physical courage, teamwork, and respect.

Vision  
To build a durable regional rugby pathway from elementary school exposure through youth club rugby, middle school and high school competition, select-side opportunities, and eventually senior amateur rugby.

Club Purpose  
Spotsylvania RFC is not being founded merely to create a team.

It is being founded to create a rugby institution that can serve families, schools, players, coaches, and the wider community for decades.

The club should become a place where young athletes learn to:  
\- play hard  
\- respect opponents  
\- control aggression  
\- grow in courage  
\- serve teammates  
\- accept coaching  
\- compete honorably  
\- develop physical confidence  
\- take responsibility  
\- understand team before self

4\. Public Motto and Internal Values

Public Motto  
Unity · Courage · Service

Use this public triad for broad-facing materials when a short, simple value expression is needed.

Internal Formation Values  
The deeper internal values are:

1\. Courage  
Rugby requires physical, moral, and social courage. Players must learn to act under pressure, support teammates, and face difficulty without panic or complaint.

2\. Discipline  
Rugby is not chaos. It requires laws, structure, restraint, timing, and self-control. Toughness without discipline is not strength.

3\. Team Before Self  
Rugby rewards support play. No player succeeds alone. The club should form athletes who understand that their effort, positioning, communication, and sacrifice affect the whole team.

4\. Respect  
Respect is owed to coaches, referees, teammates, opponents, parents, and the game itself. The club should insist on a high standard of sportsmanship.

5\. Formation  
The club exists to form people, not only athletes. Rugby should help young men and women become more responsible, resilient, honest, and generous.

6\. Local Stewardship  
Spotsylvania RFC should belong to the community it serves. The club should build relationships with schools, families, churches, parks, coaches, and local institutions.

7\. Durability  
The club must be built to last. Systems, documents, trained adults, and institutional partnerships matter as much as enthusiasm.

Culture Standard  
The club should be competitive, but not reckless.  
It should be tough, but not crude.  
It should be welcoming, but not unserious.  
It should be family-friendly, but not soft.  
It should be ambitious, but not overextended.

5\. Founding Narrative

The Problem  
The greater Fredericksburg and Spotsylvania region does not yet have a complete, durable rugby development pathway.

Young athletes may encounter football, soccer, baseball, basketball, lacrosse, and wrestling, but rugby remains largely absent from the ordinary youth sports landscape.

Where rugby does exist in similar areas, clubs often struggle because they depend on a single founder, a single coach, a single school, or a single group of families. When those people move on, the club disappears.

The Opportunity  
Spotsylvania has the ingredients for a durable rugby ecosystem:  
\- growing families  
\- strong youth sports culture  
\- access to soccer and football athletes  
\- Catholic and private school networks  
\- public school district identities  
\- regional proximity to existing rugby structures  
\- a need for off-season development sports  
\- a community that can support disciplined, physical, team-based formation

Rugby can fit this environment if it is introduced gradually, safely, and institutionally.

The Strategy  
Spotsylvania RFC will begin with youth development, not senior rugby.

The first step is to introduce non-contact rugby to elementary and middle school students through clinics, PE partnerships, and flag or tag formats.

From there, the club can develop middle school 7s, high school 7s and XVs, a county-wide select side, and eventually senior amateur XVs.

The club will grow one age band at a time.

The Long-Term Dream  
The long-term dream is a regional rugby pathway where a child might first encounter rugby in elementary PE, play flag or tag rugby in grades 3–5, transition to 7s in middle school, compete in high school rugby, represent a future Spotsylvania United select side, and eventually play senior amateur rugby for Spotsylvania RFC.

This would create not just teams, but a rugby culture.

The Founding Commitment  
Spotsylvania RFC will be built deliberately.  
It will start small.  
It will train adults.  
It will document systems.  
It will respect schools.  
It will serve families.  
It will develop players.  
It will build something that can last.

Initial Anchor  
The club may initially be anchored around The Summit Academy in Fredericksburg, Virginia.

Summit may serve as:  
\- an early institutional partner  
\- a warm community for first families  
\- a potential fiscal sponsor during the founding phase  
\- a possible future school rugby program

Important Distinction  
Summit Academy may be an early anchor and partner, but Spotsylvania RFC should be designed as a broader community club.

The relationship should remain clear:  
\- Summit may host or sponsor early activity.  
\- Summit families may provide early momentum.  
\- Summit may eventually have its own school team.  
\- Spotsylvania RFC should remain capable of serving the wider county and region.

This distinction prevents confusion later.

7\. Long-Term Structure and Development Pathway

Desired Long-Term Structure  
The desired long-term structure is a regional rugby pipeline consisting of:  
1\. Elementary school exposure  
2\. District-level youth clubs or youth hubs  
3\. Middle school rugby  
4\. High school rugby  
5\. Spotsylvania United select side  
6\. Senior amateur Spotsylvania RFC XVs

Possible Future District Clubs  
Possible future district clubs may map to Spotsylvania County school districts:  
\- Battlefield  
\- Berkeley  
\- Chancellor  
\- Courtland  
\- Lee Hill  
\- Livingston  
\- Salem

Future Select-Side Identity  
Spotsylvania United RFC may be reserved for a future county-wide or regional select side drawing from district-level youth clubs.

Do not use Spotsylvania United RFC for the founding club until the select-side concept exists operationally.

Development Spine  
K–2: Imagine Rugby / PE introduction — basic movement, passing, evasion, fun  
Grades 3–5 / U10: Flag or tag rugby — first club experience, no contact barrier  
Grade 6 / U12: Tag or tackle 7s — transition age, format depends on readiness and league structure  
Grades 7–8 / U14: Tackle 7s — middle school development  
Grades 9–12 / U16–U18: 7s and XVs — high school competition and player formation  
Select side: 7s primary — competitive regional representation  
Senior: XVs — long-term endpoint of the pathway

First-Year Strategic Wedge  
Spotsylvania RFC should not attempt to launch every age group at once.

The strongest first-year wedge is:  
Grades 3–6 non-contact rugby clinics and flag/tag rugby programming.

This gives the club the best chance to:  
\- reduce parental concern about contact  
\- enter schools through PE and enrichment programs  
\- attract soccer families  
\- introduce rugby before athletes specialize  
\- build a future middle school tackle base  
\- develop coaches gradually  
\- avoid overextending the founding team

Do not lead with cannon imagery in youth-facing, school-facing, or parent-facing communications.  
Do not use the cannon wheel outside the formal crest context without deliberate review.

Rule:  
Emphasize formation, craft, and durability. The cannon wheel is permitted in the formal crest as a heritage symbol. Avoid weapons, battle, or war framing in all other contexts.

13\. Institutional Design Principles

Principle 1: Never Build Around One Person  
The founder may start the club, but the founder cannot be the club.

Every major responsibility should eventually have:  
\- a primary owner  
\- a backup owner  
\- written instructions  
\- a repeatable calendar  
\- stored documents  
\- a successor candidate

Principle 2: Certify Parent-Coaches Early  
The club should not rely only on experienced rugby people. Parent-coaches should be treated as stakeholders, not emergency volunteers.

Principle 3: Document Everything  
Every recurring process should be documented: registration, coach certification, field reservations, school outreach, parent communication, practice plans, safety procedures, equipment lists, fixture scheduling, fundraising, and season closeout.

If a process is not documented, it is fragile.

Principle 4: Build Fixture Continuity  
A club with no fixtures becomes a clinic. A club with reliable fixtures becomes a team.

Principle 5: Secure Field Access Early  
Field access is a founding priority, not a later detail.

Principle 6: Use Schools Carefully  
Schools are essential, but they require trust. No school should be approached casually without a clear explanation of insurance, coach certification, child safety, non-contact format, supervision, schedule, facilities, parent communication, and institutional responsibility.

Principle 7: Start with Non-Contact Rugby  
The club should begin with non-contact rugby for younger players. This reduces barriers for parents, schools, new coaches, soccer families, first-time players, insurance, and facility discussions.

Principle 8: Grow by Age Band, Not by Excitement  
The better pattern is: introduce, clinic, train, register, play, repeat, expand.

Principle 9: Keep Summit and Spotsylvania RFC Distinct  
Summit may be an early anchor, but Spotsylvania RFC should remain a broader community club.

Principle 10: Build for Handoff  
Every season should end with updated contact lists, financial summary, coach list, player list, lessons learned, field notes, school notes, equipment inventory, and next-season recommendations.

14\. Strategic Roadmap Summary

Current Status  
Spotsylvania RFC is in the planning phase.

At present, the club should assume it has no formal charter, no field, no co-founders, no certified coaches, no registered players, no independent nonprofit status, and no confirmed school partnership unless those items are later confirmed and recorded.

This is not a weakness. It is the honest starting point.

Phase 1: Legitimacy  
Goal: Establish the minimum legal, insurance, and rugby-governance foundation required to speak credibly with schools, parents, and parks departments.

Phase 2: Institutional Support  
Goal: Ensure the club is not dependent on one person by recruiting founding adults, prospective parent-coaches, school liaison, field liaison, finance/admin help, and advisory support.

Phase 3: Field and School Access  
Goal: Secure a realistic place to train and a realistic path into schools.

Phase 4: Pilot Program  
Goal: Run a small, excellent, low-risk introductory rugby program.

Best Pilot Option  
A non-contact rugby clinic for grades 3–6.

Possible Format  
\- 3–4 weeks  
\- 60–75 minutes per session  
\- flag or touch format  
\- no tackling  
\- no scrums  
\- no rucks  
\- no mauls  
\- emphasis on passing, evasion, support, spacing, and fun

Phase 5: First Competitive Step  
Goal: Move from clinic to club play through an appropriate Rugby Virginia pathway, youth festival, middle school development opportunity, Summit-based after-school rugby, or local jamboree.
